Why I Challenge Hinduism as an Agnostic

As an agnostic, I question everything, including religion. I don't believe in something just because others say it's true.

As an agnostic, I question everything, including religion. I don’t believe in something just because others say it’s true. Agnosticism means I don’t know for sure if there’s a god or a higher power. I think it’s important to keep asking questions and to search for answers based on evidence, not faith.

Hinduism, like other religions, talks about gods, karma, and the cycle of life, death, and rebirth (samsara). But as an agnostic, I wonder how we can be sure these things are real when there is no proof. The stories and ideas in Hinduism, like in any religion, are based on belief, not facts we can test or see for ourselves.

Hinduism also has many gods and different teachings depending on the region or belief system. This makes it even harder to know which one is true. From my point of view, it’s hard to believe in something when we don’t have clear evidence. Just because something has been believed for a long time doesn’t mean it’s true.

I’m not saying that Hinduism or any religion is wrong, but I believe we should question everything. I believe in curiosity, exploring the unknown, and looking for real answers. I challenge Hinduism (and all religions) because I want to understand the world in a way that makes sense based on evidence, not just what we are told to believe.

If We All Thought Like This, We’d Never Do Anything Crazy!

If we all thought like this—questioning everything, being skeptical—we’d never achieve anything crazy or amazing. But that’s how we progress!

Let’s be real for a second. If everyone thought like this—questioning everything, being skeptical of everything that’s been told to us—how would humanity ever do anything insane or amazing? We wouldn’t!

I mean, seriously, look at history. If people didn’t challenge the norm, we wouldn’t have had the big breakthroughs. We wouldn’t have stepped foot on the moon. We wouldn’t be questioning the vastness of space, or diving deep into AI, or even pushing the limits of human creativity.

Imagine if the first person who looked at the stars was like, “Oh, I’m just gonna accept that gods live up there and that’s it.” We would’ve never discovered gravity or understood the universe like we do now.

But people didn’t think that way. They asked “What’s out there?” They dared to believe they could figure it out. *They did some crazy stuff—stuff that made everyone else say, “WTF?!”*

And that’s the point. If we never asked “Why?” or “What if?” we would still be stuck in the same place. If we kept following every rule or belief without questioning it, we would never move forward. So, yeah, I’m all for challenging ideas, even the ones that are thousands of years old. That’s how we grow.

Why I think Astrology is fake

Astrology suggests that celestial bodies affect our lives in a way that doesn't stand up to scientific scrutiny.

Astrology claims that the positions of stars and planets at the time of our birth determine our personality traits and future. However, there's no scientific evidence to support this idea. The forces of gravity and other influences that astrology suggests have no measurable effect on human behavior or life outcomes. Furthermore, astrologers have failed to demonstrate any repeatable, verifiable connection between astrological signs and personality traits or events in people's lives.

In essence, astrology operates on vague generalities and broad statements that could apply to anyone, which is why people often feel like astrology predictions fit them. The lack of consistency in predictions and the inability to scientifically test astrology make it more of a pseudoscience than a legitimate field of study. So, while astrology can be fun or entertaining, I think it's best to treat it with skepticism and rely on more evidence-based practices to understand our world and ourselves.
